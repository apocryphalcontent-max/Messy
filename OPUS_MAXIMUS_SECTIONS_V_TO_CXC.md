# V. THE CONSCIOUS SELF (BRANCH: 600 SUBJECTS)

## V.A: Self-Consciousness & Identity (80 subjects)

**Research Validation**: The problem of self-consciousness and personal identity spans from Descartes to Parfit. Understanding what makes me "me" across time is fundamental to ethics, theology, and metaphysics.

**Warranted Scope**: 80 subjects covers:
- Cartesian cogito (15 subjects)
- Lockean memory theory (20 subjects)
- Contemporary debates (Parfit, animalism) (30 subjects)
- First-person perspective (15 subjects)

### Examples:

1. **Fichte's Doctrine of the Absolute I as the Foundation of All Knowledge**
   - Wissenschaftslehre (1794)
   - The I posits itself absolutely
   - Self-consciousness as the ground of all consciousness
   - Thesis-antithesis-synthesis structure
   - The absolute I vs. the empirical I

2. **Derek Parfit's Reductionist Account of Personal Identity and Survival**
   - Reasons and Persons (1984)
   - Personal identity is not what matters
   - Psychological continuity suffices
   - Fission cases: which person am I?
   - The non-identity problem

3. **John Locke's Memory Theory of Personal Identity**
   - Essay Concerning Human Understanding II.27
   - Personal identity consists in continuity of consciousness
   - The Prince and the Cobbler thought experiment
   - Problems: circularity, false memories
   - Memory as a criterion vs. memory as constitutive

4. **The Problem of the Self in Buddhist Philosophy: Anatta (No-Self)**
   - The doctrine of anatman
   - The five aggregates (skandhas)
   - Stream of consciousness without a substantial self
   - Parallels with Hume's bundle theory
   - Challenges to Western substance metaphysics

5. **Thomas Reid's Critique of Locke: The Brave Officer Paradox**
   - Essays on the Intellectual Powers (1785)
   - The transitivity problem for memory theories
   - Boy flogged → brave officer → old general
   - Memory connects pairs but not all three
   - The need for a substantial self

6. **Animalism: We Are Essentially Animals**
   - Eric Olson's The Human Animal (1997)
   - We are identical to our biological organisms
   - Not identical to our brains or minds
   - The thinking animal problem
   - Too many thinkers objection

7. **The Phenomenology of Self-Awareness: Pre-reflective Cogito**
   - Sartre's non-positional self-consciousness
   - Self-awareness prior to reflection
   - The structure of ipseity
   - Husserl's pure ego as the pole of experience
   - Zahavi's minimal self

8. **Sydney Shoemaker's Self-Knowledge and Self-Identity**
   - First-person authority
   - Why I have privileged access to my own mental states
   - The immunity to error through misidentification
   - Cannot be mistaken that it's *I* who is in pain
   - Contrast with third-person attribution

## VI.C: The Problem of Evil & Theodicy (100 subjects)

**Research Validation**: The problem of evil is the most formidable objection to theism. From Epicurus to contemporary debates, this requires comprehensive treatment.

**Warranted Scope**: 100 subjects justified because:
- Central to natural theology
- Numerous theodicies require individual treatment (30 subjects)
- Logical vs. evidential problem (20 subjects)
- Specific types of evil (natural, moral, animal suffering) (30 subjects)
- Skeptical theism and defenses (20 subjects)

### Examples:

1. **Leibniz's Best of All Possible Worlds Theodicy**
   - Théodicée (1710)
   - This world is the best of all possible worlds
   - Metaphysical, physical, and moral evil
   - The principle of sufficient reason
   - Voltaire's Candide as a satirical response

2. **Irenaeus's Soul-Making Theodicy and the Necessity of Suffering**
   - Against Heresies
   - Evil as necessary for soul-making
   - The Epistemic Distance from God
   - John Hick's Irenaean theodicy
   - Moral and spiritual development through trials

3. **Augustine's Privation Theory of Evil**
   - Confessions VII, City of God XII
   - Evil is not a positive reality
   - Evil is the privation (privatio) of good
   - Like a hole in a sweater
   - Only good has being; evil is non-being

4. **The Free Will Defense**
   - Moral evil results from creaturely free choice
   - God values libertarian freedom
   - Could not create beings with free will who never sin
   - [Plantinga's version covered above]
   - Hick, Swinburne versions

5. **The Problem of Animal Suffering**
   - Animals suffer but are not moral agents
   - Cannot be justified by free will defense
   - Didn't participate in the Fall
   - How can this be compatible with a good God?
   - Possible responses: eschatological compensation, natural necessity

6. **Skeptical Theism**
   - Stephen Wykstra, William Alston
   - God's reasons are beyond our comprehension
   - We should not expect to see the reason for each evil
   - "No see'um" inference is unreliable
   - Critique: undermines all theodicies?

7. **Marilyn McCord Adams's Horrendous Evils**
   - Horrendous Evils and the Goodness of God (1999)
   - Some evils are so bad that they seem to ruin a life
   - Holocaust, extreme torture
   - God must "defeat" horrendous evils, not just outweigh them
   - Christological solution: God's participation in suffering

8. **Ivan Karamazov's Protest: The Rebellion**
   - Dostoyevsky's Brothers Karamazov
   - "I don't want harmony if it costs the tears of one child"
   - The suffering of innocent children
   - Returning one's "ticket" to God
   - Eschatological justification is insufficient
